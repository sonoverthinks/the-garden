## 1. The Two-Step Modeling Process

To construct a valid model, you must follow two distinct steps:

1. **Describe the possible outcomes:** Define the Sample Space ($\Omega$).
2. **Specify a probability law:** Assign probabilities to outcomes or sets of outcomes (events) based on beliefs or physical properties.

---

## 2. The Sample Space ($\Omega$)

The sample space is the set of all possible outcomes. For a set to be a valid sample space, it must satisfy three criteria:

- **Mutually Exclusive:** Only one outcome can occur at a time.
- **Collectively Exhaustive:** The set must cover every possible outcome of the experiment.
- **Right Granularity:** The level of detail must be relevant to the question being asked (avoiding unnecessary complexity while retaining essential data).

### Examples

- **Discrete (Finite):** Rolling two tetrahedral (4-sided) dice. The sample space consists of 16 discrete pairs: $(1,1), (1,2), \dots, (4,4)$.
- **Continuous:** Throwing a dart at a square target. The sample space is the set of coordinates $\{(x, y) \mid 0 \le x, y \le 1\}$.

---

## 3. The Probability Axioms

For a probability law to be mathematically "meaningful," it must satisfy three fundamental axioms:

1. **Non-negativity:** For any event $A$, $P(A) \ge 0$.
2. **Normalization:** The probability of the entire sample space is 1, $P(\Omega) = 1$.
3. **Additivity:** If $A$ and $B$ are disjoint events ($A \cap B = \emptyset$), then:

   $$P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B)$$

> **Note:** For continuous distributions, the probability of any _individual_ single point outcome (e.g., hitting a specific coordinate $(0.5, 0.3)$ with a dart) is $0$. Probability is instead assigned to **events** (subsets of the sample space).

---

## 4. Simple Consequences of Axioms

From the three axioms, we can derive several useful properties:

- **Upper Bound:** $P(A) \le 1$
- **Impossible Event:** $P(\emptyset) = 0$
- **Complementary Probability:** $P(A) + P(A^c) = 1$
- **General Additivity:** For $k$ disjoint events, $P(A_1 \cup \dots \cup A_k) = \sum_{i=1}^k P(A_i)$
- **Monotonicity:** If $A \subset B$, then $P(A) \le P(B)$

---

## 5. Calculation Methodology

When solving probability problems, follow this workflow:

1. **Specify the Sample Space:** Define $\Omega$.
2. **Specify the Probability Law:** Define how likelihood is distributed (often involves some assumptions, like "uniformity").
3. **Identify the Event of Interest:** Define the subset $A$. _Tip: Use a picture/diagram whenever possible._
4. **Calculate:** Sum the probabilities (discrete) or calculate the area/volume (continuous).

### Discrete Example: Two Tetrahedral Dice

Let each outcome have a probability of $1/16$.

- If $X$ is the first roll: $P(X=1) = 4 \times \frac{1}{16} = \frac{1}{4}$
- If $Z = \min(X, Y)$: $P(Z=2) = \frac{5}{16}$ (Outcomes: $(2,2), (2,3), (2,4), (3,2), (4,2)$)

### Continuous Example: Dart Board

Given a uniform probability law where **Probability = Area**:

- $P(\{(x, y) \mid x + y \le 1/2\}) = \text{Area of the resulting triangle} = \frac{1}{2} \times \frac{1}{2} \times \frac{1}{2} = \frac{1}{8}$

---

## 6. Infinite Sample Spaces & Countable Additivity

In discrete but infinite spaces (e.g., flipping a coin until "Heads" appears), we use the **Countable Additivity Axiom**:

If $A_1, A_2, A_3, \dots$ is an infinite sequence of **disjoint** events, then:

$$P\left(\bigcup_{i=1}^\infty A_i\right) = \sum_{i=1}^\infty P(A_i)$$

- **Constraint:** This only holds for _countable_ sequences.
- **Measure Theory:** The unit square or a real line is _uncountable_. Area is a legitimate law here as long as we don't try to assign probabilities to "pathological" or "strange" sets.

## 7. Interpretations of Probability

| **Perspective**  | **Description**                                                        |
| ---------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Mathematical** | A branch of math where axioms lead to theorems.                        |
| **Frequentist**  | Probability is the long-run frequency of an event (e.g., coin tosses). |
| **Bayesian**     | Probability is a subjective degree of belief or betting preference.    |
